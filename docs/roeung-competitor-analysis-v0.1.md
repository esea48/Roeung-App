# Roeung (រឿង) — Competitor Analysis
**Version:** 0.1  
**Date:** June 2026  
**Scope:** Consumer and nonprofit oral history / family memory platforms

---

## Market overview

The family memory preservation space is growing but largely English-first and Western-focused. No competitor is purpose-built for Southeast Asian diaspora communities. The closest analogues are either generic multilingual tools (Biograf) or diaspora-specific nonprofit archives (JIMENA) — neither combines the private family model, AI translation, and human curation that Roeung is built around.

---

## Competitors

### StoryWorth *(US, consumer)*
Weekly email prompts ask a family member a question; they reply in writing. After a year, responses are compiled into a printed hardcover book. Text-first, English-only. The elder must be literate and comfortable with email. No voice, no translation, no curation step.

**Pricing:** $69–$199/year (book included)

### Remento *(US, consumer)*
Voice and video prompts sent weekly; the elder records their answer via a mobile app. AI transcribes. Output is a printed book with QR codes linking to the original audio. English-only. Requires app download and a degree of tech literacy from the elder. No bilingual support, no human review step.

**Pricing:** $99/year or $12.99/month

### Biograf *(International, consumer)*
Video-first memory app supporting 35+ languages with auto-translation. Prompt-based — the app guides elders through structured questions. Private archive, not a public platform. Closest international competitor to Roeung. However: generic language coverage rather than community-specific, no Keeper/curation model, video-first (not audio), and no cultural sensitivity layer in translation.

**Pricing:** Subscription (undisclosed)

### JIMENA — "Seeing the Voices" *(International, nonprofit)*
A mobile oral history app built specifically for Mizrahi and Sephardic Jewish diaspora communities to document stories of Jewish refugees from North Africa and the Middle East. Government-funded ($2.6M initiative via Israel's Ministry of Social Equality). Stories go into a public archive, not a private family book. Relevant as a proof of concept that diaspora-specific oral history tools have genuine demand and institutional support — but not a commercial competitor.

**Pricing:** Free (nonprofit funded)

### Storied *(US, consumer)*
Genealogy-first platform that pairs family tree building with story collection. Users can build or upload a family tree, attach stories, photos, and historical records to ancestors, and compile everything into a printed book with QR codes. Collaborative — multiple family members can contribute. No voice recording or AI transcription as core features. English-only, no translation. The only competitor with a family tree as a core feature; stories are secondary to ancestry research.

**Pricing:** Free tier; paid plans from $6.99/month; StoriedBooks $99

## Where Roeung is differentiated

**Language-first, not language-bolted-on.** Every competitor treats translation as a secondary feature or ignores it entirely. Roeung is designed Khmer-first — the original language is always the source of truth, and English is the translation layer. This is the correct frame for the actual user.

**The elder does nothing.** StoryWorth requires writing. Remento, Biograf, and Storied require app navigation. JIMENA requires a guided interview setup. In Roeung, the elder just speaks. This is the only product designed for a non-literate, non-English-speaking elder as the primary subject.

**Human curation as a feature.** All consumer competitors ship AI output directly to the end product. Roeung's Keeper review step means a trusted family member always checks transcript and translation quality before a story goes live — critical for a language like Khmer where AI errors can be meaningful.

**The Keeper succession model.** No competitor has a minimum guardian requirement or succession mechanism. For a family archive intended to outlast any individual, this matters.

---

## Key risks

- **Biograf adding community-specific support** — if Biograf deepened its Khmer language support and added a review layer, it would be a more direct competitor. Currently it shows no sign of this.
- **StoryWorth or Remento adding translation** — both could bolt on a translation API. The defensible moat is cultural specificity and the UX simplicity for non-tech elders, not translation alone.
